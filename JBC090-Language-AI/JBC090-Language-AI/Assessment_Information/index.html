(Automated) Plagiarism

Assignments will be checked for plagiarism; not only the writing, the code too. The easiest way to avoid issues is by doing your own work. This means: make sure your writing is your own. Copy-pasting and paraphrasing particular words does not count as making it your own writing. Read, synthesize, write anew. Similarly, we do not recommend copying code off the Internet verbatim, or â€˜borrowingâ€™ code from other students not in your group. Both significantly increase the chances of potential plagiarism. If you do use academic work in writing, or repositories in code, make sure to cite them (in the document as a reference, or in the comments as a link).
On the use of Large Language Models (LLMs, such as ChatGPT and variants): how ever much Iâ€™d like you to encourage you to pursue your own intellectual endeavors, I am not under the illusion that I can ban you from using LLMs for your work. Feel free to use them. While doing so, however, please realize that the chances of overlap with other students using LLMs is rather high. This may potentially become a plagiarism issue between groups. Additionally, please refrain from using LLMs to â€˜improveâ€™ your writing. The research paper is not expected to be written in fancy academic English. Succinct and to the point is good; typically not something that LLMs do all that well. If you want to use them to correct spelling and grammar: no issues there.  Exam (60%)

The exam is a Schoolyear ANS exam, which will not be fully open book. In addition to your standard equipment, you are allowed to bring:
A single sheet of A4 paper worth of notes. This can be double-sided, in any font size you can still read. Equations will be given on the exam.
You can bring a standard scientific (i.e., without graphing and/or programmable capabilities) calculator.

Exam Content

Video Lectures: you are expected to understand, and to be able to apply the material in the lectures (and therefore the book) in similar detail, unless specified otherwise in the lectures.
Please note this implies that topics and algorithms in the book that are not discussed in the lecture are not part of the exam!
Exercises: additional exercises have been provided for working through the different algorithms. You may expect any of these (or at least their subcomponents, especially the more time-intensive ones) on the exam (with different values of course).
In-Person Lectures and Lab Sessions: these cover contextual content to the application of the material. Hence, you are expected to be largely familiar with the additional information provided. You are not expected to write any code on the exam, however!
Week 7â€™s in-person lecture discusses reading that is part of the exam, so this part should receive some additional attention while studying. Generally, you can expect the lab and in-person content to be supplementary and hence useful to answering questions. However, the examâ€™s focus is largely on the video lecture content.
Readings: The book is exam material (but see Lectures bullet point). In general, the reading materials (papers) provided per week are supplementary and hence their literal contents (i.e., what they provide as additional information) are not part of the exam. The only exception are papers explicitly marked to be exam material (these will also be discussed in lectures).

 Question Style

The exam consists of multiple choice, calculation (answer in box with one value/vector, full calculation does not need to be provided), and essay-style questions (answer in writing in an answer box; length of the box should be sufficient). This order is from most to least occurring (typically there are 22 questions, 2 of which are open worth 5 points, ~5 are calculation questions worth 1 pt, and the rest is MC worth 1 pt).
Roughly 1/3 of the questions will be about theory, 1/3 of the questions will be about algorithms, and 1/3 of the questions will be about applications and insights.
Points will be noted per question. For the multiple-choice questions, only one answer is correct.
The questions might, for example, look like (but are not limited to) such:
Theory: given some statements about a single concept (e.g., ambiguity, semantics, bias): choose which one applies â€” or, given a concept, explain its use and limitations.
Algorithms: given several descriptions of algorithm functionality: choose which one holds for a particular algorithm â€” or, given (part of) an algorithm and an input: calculate the correct numerical outcome (typically a value or a vector).
Applications / insights: given a description of some data science use case: choose/motivate what the best NLP pipeline would be â€” or, given some limitation of a corpus: choose/motivate what the effect would be on a certain task.
Essay-style: given a particular algorithm, describe how its performance may improved in a specific setting through use of techniques from other parts of the course.
